Korean BBQ Style tofu

Author: David Gallacher
  •  Cuisine
  • Korean
  •  Course
  • Entree/Main Course
  •  Method
  • Frying
  •  Cook Time
  • 40-50 minutes
  •  Prep Time
  • 20-30 minutes
  •  Yields
  • 3 servings
Description

I'm really not the person to discuss Korean food, but I do love the wonderful flavours and seasonings that are used in Korean cuisine. Korean dishes that are popular in North America tend to be very meat-centric, so this is a twist on the classic Korean BBQ where we use tofu as the protein instead. 

Ingredients
  • For the sauce:
  • 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 2 tbsp dark soy sauce
  • Juice of half of a lemon (~1-2 tbsp)
  • 1 tsp sesame seeds
  • 2 tsp gochugaru (Korean chilli pepper)
  • 1 tsp red chilli flakes
  • 1 tsp Sichuan pepper, ground
  • 1 tbsp maple syrup
  • 2 tbsp granulated sugar
  • 1 tbsp corn starch , 2 tbsp water (for slurry)
  • 1/2 cup finely diced white/yellow onion (~1/2 of a medium onion)
  • 1 inch nub of ginger, grated
  • 2 cloves of garlic, finely chopped
  • 1/4 tsp MSG
  • 1/4 tsp Chinese five spice
  • For the dish:
  • 1 block of tofu, frozen then thawed (Freezing the tofu the night before then thawing in the morning gives the tofu a very nice texture for meat-substitution) 
  • 1/2 cup of corn starch to coat tofu
  • 1-2 cups of canola/vegetable oil for frying
  • 1 1/2 cup of long grained white rice
  • 4-5 oyster mushrooms
  • 5-6 small-medium sized carrots
Instructions
  • First we will prepare the sauce, in a medium sized pan add a small amount of neutral oil and sauté onion, garlic and ginger until blonde, at medium heat
  • While sautéing onion and garlic mixture, in a small bowl mix together tomato paste, sugar, vinegar, soy sauce, lemon juice, maple syrup, MSG and sesame seeds 
  • Once onion mixture is blonde, add Sichuan pepper, gochugaru, red chilli flakes and five spice and bloom spices
  • Add liquids to pan and bring to a simmer, reduce heat and add cornstarch slurry
  • Allow sauce to reduce to desired thickness then remove from heat and set aside 
  • Next we will prepare the tofu. Remove block from package and drain liquid. At this stage you can press your tofu to remove excess moisture, but since we're frying it's nice to keep a bit of moisture inside the tofu. I typically compress the tofu in my hands over the sink for a few seconds to remove most of the liquid 
  • Tear the tofu with your hands into ~1 " pieces, and toss in a large bowl with corn starch. Season with salt and pepper to taste
  • In a large pot, bring ~1" of oil to 375 ℉ to shallow fry the tofu* 
  • Add coated tofu to oil and fry for 6-7 minutes or until golden, stirring occasionally to ensure all surfaces get cooked evenly. Remove tofu, draining excess oil  
  • Toss tofu in sauce and serve with rice and desired sides
  • For this dish, I served with fried oyster mushrooms and roasted carrots. To fry oyster mushrooms I place sliced mushrooms in a large pan at medium heat with a small amount of olive oil, then using a pot stainless steel pot lid I compress the mushrooms so they are flattened, and fry until crispy, flipping halfway. This produces really nice crispy and chewy mushrooms that are full of umami flavours
  • Enjoy!
Notes
  •  * See note in orange cauliflower recipe about shallow frying for more information

Dutch Apple Pie

Author: David Gallacher
  •  Cuisine
  • Dutch/American
  •  Course
  • Desert
  •  Method
  • Baking
  •  Cook Time
  • 50-60 Minutes
  •  Prep Time
  • 1 hour
  •  Yields
  • 6 servings
Description

Dutch apple pie combines the best of apple crumble with apple pie, and is the perfect desert to make in the fall during apple picking season. I prefer granny smith or other less sweet apples to balance all the sugar we're going to be adding to the pie. We will remove the peels, but they can be recycled for apple jelly, or as a great source of pectin for other jams and preserves you may want to make in the fall.

Ingredients
  • For the topping:
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar
  • 1/2 cup cold butter cubes
  • 1/2 cup rolled or steel cut oats
  • 1/2 cup flour
  • 1 tsp vanilla
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • For the filling:
  • 4-5 medium sized grannie smith apples, peeled and cut into 1/4" thick slices
  • 1/2 cup rolled or steel cut oats
  • 1/2 cup flour
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar
  • 1/4 cup white sugar
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 tsp ground cloves
  • 1 tbsp lemon juice*
  • For the pastry dough:
  • 2 cups flour
  • 2 tbsp sugar
  • 1/2 tsp salt (if using unsalted butter)
  • 6 tbsp vegan butter stick **
  • 7-8 tsbp ice water 
  • 7-8 tsbp ice water 
Instructions
  • We prepare the dough first because it needs to rest for a little bit. Start by combining flour, sugar and salt in a large bowl. Then place mixture in freezer with utensils to chill
  • Grate butter into flour and mix until the texture is sandy, careful not to over mix, we want solid pieces of butter to melt into the dough. After mixing place back into freezer to chill
  • Add ice water 1-2 tbsp at a time to mixture, gently stirring until dough is combined and can be formed into a solid ball
  • If flour remains in the bowl, add 1-2 tbsp of ice water until it forms a ball
  • Let dough chill in the refrigerator for 20-30 minutes, covered
  • Now we will prepare the filling, peel and core apples and discard (or keep and freeze) skins. After peeling cut the apples into 1/4" thick slices. To keep apples from browning during this process, place apples into a bowl of cold water with lemon juice
  • In a large bowl, combine flour, oats, sugar and seasonings. Mix well then add apple slices and stir until apples are all coated, if mixture is too dry add lemon juice for moisture.
  • Now we will prepare the topping, soften butter first (10-15 seconds in the microwave works well) and in another bowl add all topping ingredients and stir until combined
  • Remove the ball of dough from the fridge and roll out into a ~12" circle, we will trim the edges so the circle does not need to be perfect
  • Place dough inside a standard 9" pie dish, pressing down bottom and edges 
  • The next step depends on you, for a crispy bottom crust it is recommended to do a so-called "blind" bake. To do this, first trim the edges of your dough to size and pinch edges for a rustic looking pie crust, and puncture the dough with a fork to allow steam to release. Next place a piece of aluminum foil inside the pie, pressing down until it forms into the pie shape, then fill with ~2-3 cm of dried beans (any kind of bean will work), this will weigh down the crust and prevent it from rising during the bake. Next bake the dough + beans at 375 ℉ for 30 minutes or until edges are brown crispy. †    
  • If we haven't done a blind bake, next we trim the edges of the dough and pinch at ~1" separations across the perimeter of the pie to form a rustic looking edge. Then puncture the dough with a fork across the bottom to allow steam to release.  
  • Place the filling inside the pie shell, until ~7/8ths full. For an attractive crust, I like to brush with a bit of melted butter and sprinkle with granulated sugar.
  • Spread the topping evenly over the top of the filling, and sprinkle with a bit of cinnamon and sugar to finish.
  • Bake the pie at 375 ℉ for 50-60 minutes or until the crust/topping is golden brown and filling is steaming. 
  • Set aside and let cool for an hour before serving.
  • Enjoy a slice of pie! (Maybe with a nice scoop of vanilla ice cream?)
Notes
  •   * Lemon juice is used for preventing apples from peeling
  •   ** Margarine is not an okay substitute here, it's important that the fat we use here is stable at room temperature, so vegan butter or vegetable shortening are ideal.
  •   † We can re-use the beans here so let them cool after baking and use for your next blind bakes!

Orange Cauliflower

Author: David Gallacher
  •  Cuisine
  • Chinese, American Chinese
  •  Course
  • Dinner
  •  Method
  • Stovetop, Frying
  •  Cook Time
  • 30 minutes
  •  Prep Time
  • 1 hour
  •  Yields
  • 3 servings
Description

Orange "chicken", made with battered cauliflower instead, this dish is a veganized version of the American Chinese dish, popularized by Panda Express. We're using freshly squeezed oranges for extra flavour, but store-bought OJ works as a fine substitute. This dish is sweet and sour, with small notes of spice, and we serve with over rice. 

Ingredients
  • For the cauliflower: 
  • 1 large cauliflower head, cut into 1-2" florets
  • 1/3 cup flour for dusting
  • For the batter:
  • 1/2 cup flour
  • 1/2 cup corn starch
  • 3/4 cup soda water*
  • 1 tsp baking powder
  • 1 tsp garlic powder
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • For the sauce:
  • 1 cup of freshly squeezed OJ (4-5 medium navel oranges)
  • Zest of 2 oranges
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce, or tamarind
  • 2 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1 tbsp corn starch +  2 tsbp water, to form a slurry
  • 2 garlic cloves, finely chopped
  • 1" nub of ginger, grated
  • 1/2 cup sugar
  • 1 tsp chili (Chili flakes, gochugaru or other chili powders all work fine)
  • 1 tsp ginger powder
Instructions
  • We start by making the sauce, so that it can cool while the cauliflower is frying. In a sauce pan, at medium heat, add a small amount of a neutral oil (Canola, veg, grapeseed etc.) and the garlic and grated ginger, removing skin from ginger before grating 
  • Cook ginger and garlic until blonde, then add chili and ginger powder to bloom**
  • Add OJ, soy, vinegar, sugar and orange zest to pan and mix well. Allow to simmer at medium heat for 2-3 minutes. 
  • In a small bowl, add cornstarch and water and mix well until it forms a slurry, mix again before adding to sauce. After adding cornstarch slurry to sauce, bring down heat to low and allow sauce to simmer for 5-10 minutes or until desired viscosity is reached, for a thicker sauce, simmer longer. Once finished remove from heat and set aside until cauliflower is prepared
  • Before we proceed with the batter we will heat the oil, I prefer to do a so-called "shallow fry" where the oil does not fully cover the cauliflower. The advantage with this method is that it uses much less oil, making it more affordable as well as much safer (Hot oil is very dangerous so be careful). In a large pot (the largest you have probably), preferably one with a thick bottom (I like to use a cast-iron dutch oven for this), add ~2-3" of oil and heat to 375 ℉ †
  • Next we will prepare the batter for the cauliflower. Add all the batter ingredients together and whisk until smooth, adding wet ingredients to dry
  • Toss cauliflower in batter in small batches, only adding the amount of cauliflower that will fit in your pot of oil (Do not overfill the pot, this will cause the oil to cool too much and you will end up with oily soggy cauliflower)
  • In small batches, add the cauliflower to the pot of oil, frying for 4-5 minutes then flipping to fry the other side. Keep an eye on the temperature of the oil, you will need to increase the heat of the pot while cooking, and decrease afterwards when preparing the next batch. When each batch of cauliflower is done transfer to a sheet pan with paper towels to remove excess oil. 
  • Once all cauliflower is fried, transfer to a bowl with the sauce and toss to coat evenly
  • Serve cauliflower immediately with rice and garnish with scallion greens and sesame seeds
  • Enjoy!
Notes
  •  * Still water works fine as well, but leads to a slightly denser coating
  •  ** Blooming spices is an essential step for getting the most flavour out of your seasoning, usually done as one of the last steps before adding wet ingredients to a sauce, you add the spices to the hot oil and allow them to cook for 1-2 minutes. This will usually lead to spices stuck to the surface of your pan, thats okay, don't try to scrape them off, add a small amount of water, vinegar, or alcohol (depending on the dishes flavour profile) to "deglaze" this will lift off the spices from the pan and allow them to be incorporated into your sauce. 
  •  † If you don't have an oil thermometer you can use the end of a wooden spoon, or a bit of batter to test that the oil is hot enough. If the end of the spoon bubbles when inserted into the oil it is ready, similarly with batter if the batter begins to fry immediately then you're good to go.  One disadvantage to doing a shallow fry is the sensitivity to temperature changes, with a much smaller volume of oil compared to deep frying, the oil will cool down quickly when you add the cauliflower. You will need to monitor the temperature carefully throughout the process and cook in smaller batches to maintain an even temperature.

Homemade Pizza

Author: David Gallacher
  •  Cuisine
  • Italian, Italian American
  •  Course
  • Dinner, Lunch
  •  Method
  • Bake
  •  Cook Time
  • 10-15 minutes
  •  Prep Time
  • 4 hours
  •  Yields
  • 1x 12" pizza
Description

Does pizza need describing? This recipe uses all-purpose dough, which gives a fluffier crust, but can be substituted with bread or Italian '00' flour for a stretchier dough that can be stretched thinner and yields a crispier pizza. Top with whatever toppings you like, this version has tomatoes and fresh basil

Ingredients
  • For the dough:
  • 2 cups all purpose flour + more for rolling and kneading 
  • 3/4 cup water, warm (~110 F)
  • 3/4 tsp salt
  • 2 tsp sugar
  • 2 tbsp olive oil + more
  • 1 packet instant yeast
  • For the sauce*:
  • 1 can crushed tomatoes
  • 1 tbsp tomato paste
  • 2-3 garlic cloves, finely chopped
  • 1 small onion, diced
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 1 tbsp dried oregano
  • 1 tbsp dried basil
  • 1/2 tsp dried chili pepper flakes
  • 1/2 tsp freshly cracked black pepper
  • 1/4 cup olive oil
  • Dry white wine to deglaze
  • For toppings:
  • 1-2 roma tomatoes, sliced
  • 3-4 sundried tomatoes, julienned
  • 2 tbsp fresh basil, julienned
  • 1 cup vegan cheese, shredded (I prefer Vialife blocks, which I grate myself because I find that pre-grated cheese doesn't tend to melt as well) 
Instructions
  • Since the dough will take the longest, we start with it. Add flour and salt into a large bowl and mix together
  • In a separate bowl, add warm water, sugar and yeast packet, stir and wait 10 minutes or until foamy, do not distrub.
  • In two stages, add olive oil, and water+yeast mixture into bowl of flour and gently mix. Add more flour if required, dough should be sticky but manageable, pulling away from the sides of the bowl 
  • Place dough on floured workstation, gently kneading for 1-2 minutes 
  • Drizzle olive oil in a separate large bowl, add dough and cover with a damp tea towel. Set aside in a warm space**, and let rise for 30-45 minutes, until dough doubles in size, resist the urge to peek at the dough, this will disturb/cool the dough
  • While the dough is rising, pre-heat the oven to 450 °F (235 °C), and begin the sauce.
  • Add onions, garlic to half the olive oil and simmer on medium-low heat until blonde
  • Add chilli peppers and black pepper and cook for 1-2 minutes to bloom, deglaze with a splash of dry white wine (If on hand, otherwise it's okay to skip)
  • Add crushed tomatoes, paste, sugar, and dried herbs and remaining olive oil, stir until mixed well, bring to a boil for 2-3 minutes then reduce heat to low and let simmer for 30-45 minutes † 
  • When the dough has doubled in size, remove from bowl and transfer to a floured surface. Gently press the dough into a large circle, if desired use a rolling-pin to work the dough into a 12" circle. If the dough does not yield easily, let it rest for 10-15 minutes covered with cling-film and try again after  
  • The next steps vary based on the equipment you have.  If you have a pizza stone, you should place it in the oven to warm up to 450 °F, if you don't have a pizza stone, a large cast-iron pan works well, or a thick sheet pan. By heating the pan first, we're emulating the cooking surface of a hot wood-oven, where the base of the oven is doing most of the cooking and can reach > 900 °F. This gives great rise to the dough, and creates a fluffy dough with a well seared crust. If you don't have a pizza stone or suitable pan, it's fine to build the pizza onto a large sheet-pan     
  • Either on a pan, or on a pizza-peel, build the pizza with your desired toppings. Roll the edges of the dough into a ~1/2" crust, before adding pizza sauce, poke small holes (not through the dough) over the dough to prevent bubbling. Add the pizza sauce and desired toppings, leaving fresh herbs/greens for after the bake 
  • Transfer to pizza stone/cast-iron or place tray into oven and bake at 450 °F for 10-12 minutes on the middle rack or until crust and toppings are golden brown.   
  • After baking, let rest for a few minutes before cutting, then serve and enjoy!
Notes
  •   * Feel free to substitute store-bought pizza sauce to save time, this will produce several pizza's worth of sauce, I recommend freezing it after use for re-use later (Allow it to cool completely before freezing)
  •   ** I was recently was advised that the inside of a clothes dryer makes an excellent make-shift proofing drawer. I've tried this several times and have been very happy with the results! Just run the dryer for 10-15 minutes before placing bowl of dough inside. Don't start the dryer after!
  •  † I like to reduce the sauce in the oven to ensure an even heating, to do this, use an all metal-pot and place in the oven at ~300 F and leave for 1 hour, stirring occasionally
